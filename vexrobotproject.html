Autonomous VEX Robot

Tools: Robot C • AVR C • Sensors • Stepper motor control • Testing

Problem Definition

Scenario: Ocean Networks Canada (ONC) experiences issues with falling debris, sedimentation, and biofouling interfering with underwater cameras and sensors. The objective was to design an autonomous system capable of navigating toward a target and accurately deploying an object using onboard sensors and actuators, under tight spatial and reliability constraints.

Design Concepts

  • Design 1: Four-wheel platform with IR and ultrasonic sensors and a motor-driven claw mechanism for object release.
  • Design 2: Car-inspired chassis focused on maneuverability, using a triangular drop mechanism and taped wheels to reduce cost.
  • Design 3: Forklift-style concept using a two-pronged lifting fork actuated by a single motor.

Each concept was evaluated based on chassis stability, sensor placement, reliability, and deployment accuracy.

Sensor Research & Selection

  • Ultrasonic sensor: Used for wall avoidance by emitting a sound pulse and measuring echo return time.
  • Infrared (IR) sensor: Used to detect the target IR light source and trigger object deployment.

This multi-sensor approach allowed robust navigation while maintaining simple control logic suitable for a microcontroller-based system.

Testing & Iteration

  • Initial claw design caused the object to be knocked off target during release; the arm was raised to correct the issue.
  • Early wheel configurations lacked stability; additional support and revised motor placement improved handling.
  • Tape was applied to wheel treads in Design 2 to smooth turning behavior and reduce slipping.

Testing highlighted the importance of mechanical alignment and actuation timing in achieving consistent performance.

Design Evaluation

A weighted objectives chart was used to quantitatively compare designs based on chassis design, sensor integration, claw effectiveness, and positioning accuracy.

  • Design 1: Highest overall score due to balanced mechanical simplicity and sensor performance.
  • Design 2: Strong maneuverability but reduced precision during turns.
  • Design 3: Conceptually robust but mechanically complex.

Final selection favored Design 1 for reliability and ease of implementation.

Results

  • Successfully demonstrated autonomous navigation toward an IR target.
  • Reliable object deployment without target interference.
  • Improved repeatability through mechanical and sensor refinements.

Lessons Learned

  • Small mechanical offsets can significantly affect deployment accuracy.
  • Sensor fusion improves robustness but requires careful calibration.
  • Early prototyping accelerates identification of failure modes.
